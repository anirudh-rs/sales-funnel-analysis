This analysis processes 109 million real events across 5.3 million users to find out. The answer is surprising: the barrier to purchase is not price. It never was.

01

The 7-stage funnel

The dataset contains three raw event types: view, cart, and purchase. Seven behavioural stages were derived from these. The most striking finding is the view-to-cart gap — 80.69% of all users who viewed a product never added anything to cart. That single stage accounts for 4,095,942 lost potential customers.

The engagement gap, not the price gap. Converted users averaged $209.70 cart price vs $208.06 for abandoned — a difference of $1.64. Price is not the barrier. Converted users had 40% more views (60 vs 42.8) and twice the cart events (4.6 vs 2.3). The barrier is engagement depth, not cost.

The obstacles — preprocessing pipeline

Before any analysis could run, five categories of data quality problems were identified and resolved entirely in SQL. The preprocessing pipeline — not the charts — is the core technical work of this project.
Step 03 · Null handling
Missing values filled
50.7M
category_code and brand nulls filled with placeholder values. Dropping 32% of rows would have destroyed funnel validity.
Step 04 · Deduplication
Duplicate events removed
130,750
Cart event flooding — worst case 78 identical cart events in one session. ROW_NUMBER() with ctid used to avoid doubling disk usage.
Step 05 · Date normalisation
Timezone split resolved
2 offsets
-04:00 EDT (Oct) and -05:00 EST (Nov). US DST changed Nov 3. All converted to UTC. 7 derived date columns stored.
Step 06 · Outlier detection
Price outliers flagged
9.87M
IQR method — price data is right-skewed. Upper fence $797.83. Flagged not deleted — 163K legitimate high-value purchases above fence.
Step 07 · Master clean
vw_clean_events created
109.8M
Single reusable view adding funnel_stage, price_tier, day_name and month_name. All 5 analysis scripts build from this view.

8.7GB→128MB

Weekly revenue & WoW change

9 weeks of data show a stable baseline of $28–37M per week — then a single anomalous week that changes everything. November 11, 2019 was Singles Day. Combined with early Black Friday promotions, that one week generated $67.8M — 21.53% of the entire 9-week revenue total.

Singles Day effect. +116.94% revenue WoW. Cart abandonment hit 58.51% that week — users added items but waited for Black Friday. Nov 18 shows deferred purchases completing, then the funnel returns to baseline by Nov 25.

Revenue distribution

82.4% of all revenue comes from products priced above $200. Budget items ($0–50) account for only 1.62% of revenue despite heavy browsing. This business lives and dies by premium electronics — and the top 10% of buyers alone generate nearly half of all revenue.

07

5 recommendations — backed by data

These are not generic best practices. Each recommendation is derived directly from the analysis with a specific number attached. Ordered by estimated revenue impact.
Priority 01
Fix the view-to-cart gap
80.69% of viewers never cart. Improve product discovery, search filtering and recommendation algorithms. This single stage is responsible for 4M lost potential buyers.
4,095,942 users
Priority 02
Recover cart abandoners
454,687 users carted but never purchased. Price is NOT the barrier — avg cart price is identical ($209 vs $208). Focus on re-engagement emails and session continuity, not discounts.
$94M potential
Priority 03
Invest in weekend campaigns
Sunday converts at 13.26% vs Friday 7.61% — a 70% difference. Shift paid ad spend toward weekend slots for the same budget with significantly higher returns.
70% uplift
Priority 04
Protect top 10% of buyers
65,524 users generate $157M — 49.83% of all revenue. They average 8.2 purchases and browse 5.1 categories. A loyalty programme for this group has exceptionally high ROI.
$157M revenue
Priority 05
Feature Apple prominently
Apple generates $83.56 revenue per viewer vs Samsung $49.42 — 69% more valuable per visit. Homepage and campaign placement should reflect this efficiency gap.
